Unit 11 Reflection


As technologies such as machine learning and artificial intelligence are advancing the question of how such technologies will be used in the future remains. What is the fate of aggregated data collected by all of us using out IoT devices, mobile phones, social media platforms. and the internet? The internet which at one point was seen as the medium to provide access to information for the repressed and to give the possibility to any of us to have our own voice , seems to become a dystopia dominated by a few companies controlling most of the access to our own data. In this strange world what will be the role for technologies like machine learning and artificial intelligence? Looking at more authoritarian regimes like China, we know that surveilance at a mass level belongs to one of the strongest weapons in the arsenal of the CCP. Traditionally such surveilance required extensive resources and participation of the public either in the form of secret police organisations or by employing grassroots informants (Pan, 2020). In the age of digital surveilance such mechanisms are supplemented by the data we provide (willingly or unwillingly) by using our mobile devices, social media platforms, communication applications etc. Aggregated data can lead to powerful inference capabilities and a disadvantage against the citizens who are unaware of the new aggregation capabilities of the state and novel techniques for mass surveillance (Pan,2020). To quote directly Meng Jianzhu, the former secretary of the Chinese Central Political and Legal Affairs “AI will drastically improve the predictability, accuracy and efficiency of social management. (Pan, 2020, p170). And if more authoritarian regimes use such techniques for mass surveillance what is really happening in the rest of the world? As citizens do we really have sufficient transparency and visibillity into how far our own governments are using large scale data aggregations for their own agendas and purposes? Are regulatory initiatives such as GDPR really in a position to address such abuses of power or is technology advancing at a pace which makes it impossible to keep up with such developments from a regulatory perspective?

To bring some counter arguments in the discussion it is a know fact that AI and ML technologies are (for the time being at least) not perfect. And even if we would have the perfect algorithms in place we need to understand not only the technologies involved but also the dynamics of authoritatian politics in the long run. As Jan puts it (Jan,2020):


 "If we believe everything we read in the news, we might believe that our world is on the verge of a dramatic and possibly terrible change because of artifical intelligence and new computational technologies. If we read political science research, aside from research on social media, computational technologies might as well not matter"

And it seems that the truth is somewhere in the middle. Much like promises made in other technological areas, it is only few times in which the technological adoption velocity matches reality. Few years ago we were promised that in the future cars will drive us around on their own and that vertical take-off and landing vehicles could be a daily reality in our cities. The reality is rather different of course with significant advances for autonomous vehicles which however encounter strong difficulties in the regulatory field of safety. In the end it is important to ask those questions and remain critical of extravagant promises but also reject the complacent narrative in which the world never changes.
