Reflective Activity 2

In 2018, Cambridge Analytica was in the news in the United Kingdom and the USA (Confessore, 2018) for obtaining and sharing data obtained from millions of Facebook users. They obtained the data through innocuous surveys on Facebook (you may have seen this type of survey and probably participated at times). This is probably the highest profile of surveys used for alternative means and, probably, monetary gains. However, this happens often through various media.

Consider how exactly this happened and why it was used. Find one or two further examples of inappropriate use of surveys and highlight the impact of all these examples from the various ethical, social, legal and professional standpoints that apply.

In 2018, Cambridge Analytica (CA) came under intense scrutiny for its role in harvesting the personal data of millions of Facebook users without their explicit consent. This data collection was facilitated through seemingly harmless Facebook surveys created by Dr. Aleksandr Kogan, a researcher from Cambridge University. Users who participated in the survey were unwittingly granting access to not only their personal data but also the data of their Facebook friends, a loophole in Facebook's API at the time (Cadwalladr & Graham-Harrison, 2018). The primary intention behind this massive data collection effort was to develop detailed psychological profiles of users to serve targeted political advertisements, thereby potentially swaying voters in a particular direction. This raised ethical concerns around informed consent, personal privacy, and the manipulation of democratic processes.

Another notorious instance of inappropriate survey usage was when the US retailer Target predicted a teen's pregnancy through her purchasing patterns, leading them to send targeted baby product ads, thereby inadvertently revealing her condition to her family (Duhigg, 2012). This instance underscores the potency of data analytics, where seemingly unrelated data points can reveal deeply personal information.

From an ethical standpoint, both examples highlight the potential for gross invasions of privacy. Not only is personal data collected without clear consent, but it is also used in ways the individuals never anticipated. Socially, these events contribute to an atmosphere of mistrust towards tech companies and surveys, as individuals grow wary of how their data will be used. Legally, such data breaches and misuses prompt demands for stricter data protection laws. The General Data Protection Regulation (GDPR) in the EU is a notable response, aiming to give individuals better control over their data (Voigt & Von dem Bussche, 2017). Professionally, these incidents emphasize the necessity for companies and researchers to prioritize data ethics and transparency, as reputational damage from such events can be severe.
